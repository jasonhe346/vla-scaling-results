Task Difficulty Ranking

Drawer (79%) > Stove (73%) >> Butter (36-42%)

Drawer is the easiest task — a single short-horizon action (open a drawer). Stove requires a two-step sequence (turn on stove, move pot). Butter requires precise pick-and-place with a small object. This aligns with the LIBERO benchmark design: LIBERO-Goal tasks are simpler than LIBERO-10 tasks, which are simpler than the manipulation-heavy LIBERO-Object tasks.

Company edges out Ego-10K on drawer & stove; tied on butter

Company wins on drawer (+2-4pp) and stove at 25K (+4.1pp), while Ego-10K wins on stove at 50K (+3.5pp). On butter, the differences (0.9-1.9pp) are within seed variance and effectively tied. The meaningful Company advantage is concentrated on the drawer task.

This is notable since Ego-10K is curated egocentric manipulation data, while Company data is from a different (potentially less curated) source. The similar or slightly better performance from Company data suggests data quality/curation may matter less than quantity at this scale, or that both sources provide sufficiently diverse visual features.

25K wins 5 out of 6 — Less pretraining is generally better

25K steps is sufficient for pretraining on 66hr of video data. In 5 of 6 task×data comparisons, 25K matches or beats 50K. The sole exception is Ego-10K on stove (+4.4pp for 50K), which may reflect that stove's longer-horizon nature benefits from more temporally diverse features.

This aligns with the earlier "inverted U" finding from butter experiments: pretraining peaks early and longer training doesn't help (and can hurt). The practical implication is significant — training for 25K steps instead of 50K halves the pretraining compute cost with no loss in downstream performance.

9. Key Findings & Analysis

1. Pretraining consistently helps on novel tasks

This is the strongest finding: all 8 pretrained conditions beat baseline on both drawer and stove. This was not guaranteed — prior butter-only experiments showed inconsistent gaps. The cross-task experiment confirms pretraining provides a reliable +3 to +9pp improvement when transferring to new tasks.

2. Company data slightly outperforms Ego-10K

Company wins 5/6 head-to-head comparisons. Average advantage: +1.6pp across all conditions. This challenges the assumption that curated egocentric manipulation data is inherently better for robot pretraining.

3. 25K pretraining steps is the sweet spot

25K beats 50K in 5/6 comparisons. Average advantage: +1.2pp for 25K over 50K. This means half the compute budget achieves better results — a clear efficiency win.

4. Pretraining benefit scales with task difficulty (inverted)

Easier tasks (drawer: 79% baseline) show the largest absolute gaps (+9.1pp best). Harder tasks (butter: ~37% baseline) show near-zero pretraining benefit. This suggests pretraining helps refine already-learnable features rather than unlocking fundamentally new capabilities. The bottleneck for butter is likely fine-tuning sample efficiency on a precision task, not feature quality.

5. Training losses don't predict eval performance

All conditions (baseline and pretrained) converge to identical training losses within each task (drawer: ~0.07, stove: ~0.10, butter: ~0.095). The pretraining advantage manifests purely in downstream evaluation, not in lower fine-tuning loss. This implies pretraining creates better initial representations that maintain their advantage through fine-tuning without being detectable via loss.

6. Pretraining reduces seed variance

Across drawer and stove, pretrained models have tighter per-seed ranges than baselines. Drawer baseline range = 6.4pp; best pretrained (Ego-10K 50K) range = 4.8pp. Stove baseline range = 19.6pp; best pretrained (Company 50K) range = 8.0pp. Pretraining makes fine-tuning more robust to random initialization.
