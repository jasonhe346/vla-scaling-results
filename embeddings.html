How pretraining reshapes the SigLIP vision encoder's representation space — comparing base, Ego-10K pretrained, and Company pretrained backbones across 5 data sources

Section 1

UMAP Visualizations

Each plot below shows 2,275 images projected into 2D using UMAP. Every image is passed through the SigLIP vision encoder (the "eyes" of the model), which produces a 2048-dimensional vector summarizing what the image looks like. UMAP then compresses those 2048 dimensions down to 2 so we can plot them.

How to read these plots: Each dot is one image. Dots that are close together mean the vision encoder sees those images as visually similar. Dots far apart mean the encoder sees them as unrelated. The colors represent the 5 data sources: Ego-10K (blue), Company (red), LIBERO Drawer (green), LIBERO Stove (yellow), LIBERO Butter (purple).

Important caveat: UMAP compresses 2048 dimensions into 2, which inevitably distorts distances. Two clusters that look "far" in 2D might actually be closer in the original space, and vice versa. Use the plots for qualitative cluster structure, not precise distance measurements. The cosine similarity numbers in Section 2 are the quantitative measure.

Section 2

Cosine Similarity

What is cosine similarity?

Every image gets turned into a 2048-dimensional vector by the vision encoder. Cosine similarity measures how similar two vectors are — it ranges from −1 (opposite directions) to +1 (same direction), with 0 meaning completely unrelated.

Think of it as asking: "How similar does the vision encoder think these two sets of images are?" If both sets show similar scenes (e.g., two factory floors), the cosine similarity is high. If one set shows a forest and the other shows a factory, it's low.

How we calculate it

For each pair of data sources (e.g., Ego-10K vs LIBERO Drawer):

  • Take all ~500 images from source A, pass each through the vision encoder, get 500 vectors of size 2048
  • Average those 500 vectors into a single "centroid" vector — this represents the typical image from that source
  • Do the same for source B
  • Compute cosine similarity between the two centroid vectors — one number summarizing how aligned the two sources are

What the numbers tell us

Before pretraining: The base SigLIP encoder sees egocentric video and LIBERO robot scenes as completely unrelated (cosine similarity ≈ 0). It's like the encoder has never seen anything resembling a robot arm, so it treats those images as being in a totally different category.

After pretraining: Similarity jumps to 0.5–0.7. The encoder now recognizes shared structure — objects being manipulated, spatial layouts, grasping motions — even though the pretraining data has no robot in it. This is the mechanism by which pretraining helps downstream task performance.

Key takeaways from the numbers

  • Pretraining data ↔ LIBERO tasks go from ~0 to 0.5–0.7: The biggest change. Before pretraining, the encoder sees no connection between egocentric video and robot sim. After pretraining, they're significantly aligned.
  • Drawer ↔ Stove drops from 0.995 to 0.72: Before pretraining, the encoder couldn't tell these tasks apart (both just "rendered kitchen"). After pretraining, it distinguishes them by action-relevant features, which is beneficial for task-specific fine-tuning.
  • Company pretraining produces higher alignment to Drawer and Stove than Ego-10K pretraining (0.71 vs 0.63 for Drawer, 0.53 vs 0.42 for Stove). Factory manipulation data is structurally closer to tabletop robot tasks.
  • Butter is the exception: Ego-10K has slightly higher alignment to Butter (0.60) than Company (0.52), and downstream performance on Butter is roughly tied between the two.
